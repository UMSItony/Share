Visualization 1: Box Plots, 5-Number Summary¶

A Box Plot of each column in the Strava data will give a summary of the non-grouped, continuous data. This summary most notably incldues the 5-number summary: the minimum value, the maximum value, the mean and the first and third quartiles for the data. While a histogram would show more detailed information about the data distribution, a box plot is the best place to start because it can help make decisions about how to group data to uncover any variations such as a particular skew direction. What little information is lost by choosing box plots in lieu of histograms is regained by using an argument in our Matplotlib's box plots to display the mean in addition to the median.

At this point in the exploration, we are attempting to assess some of the claims made by the manufacturer of one of the monitors used to produce the exercise data we are exploring. According to the claims, the device helps in the detection of "increases [or decreases, presumably] in fitness and efficiency."* Since these claims pertain to variation across multiple sessions of exercise, we can group our data by these sessions and transform each variable by taking the mean of each of the variables within each session. While this approach is not valid for future exploration of this data, it simplifies our visualization tasks and meets our goals where we are simply interested in how each variable (column of data) varies in relation to other variables across sessions (or time).

*https://www.stryd.com/guide

While we cannot be certain, the Monday and Thursday distance and enhanced_speed seem more indicative of a cycling exercise than that of a running exercise. To bolster this assertion, we can turn to two other variables that have wide ranges in the boxplots above, Air Power and Cadence. These variables were monitored with a device that is generally more suitable for running exercises rather than cycling - and data seems absent on Monday and Thursday in plots of these variables as seen above.

Other information gleaned from this exploration include:

  • Week-end runs are, on average, longer than runs at the beginning of the week.
  • Saturday runs are, on average, faster than runs on other days.
  • Monday exercises are, on average, longer and faster than those on Thursday (or any other day of the week, for that matter)
